A septic tank riser kit is a cylindrical extension, plastic or concrete, that raises your tank's access point from below the soil up to ground level, so a pumping crew can open the tank without digging. A complete kit costs $100 to $300 for DIY installation, or $300 to $600 including professional labor. Because a buried lid usually costs $50 to $200 extra in digging fees every time a truck comes out, a riser typically pays for itself within two or three service visits.

Most tanks have two access points, one at the inlet baffle and one at the outlet, and many owners install a riser on both at the same time to save on labor rather than paying for two separate visits. Once it's in, the digging fee disappears from every future pumping, inspection, and filter-cleaning appointment for as long as the riser lasts.

## What is a septic tank riser and why does it matter?

A riser is a hollow cylinder that sits on top of the tank's existing manhole opening and extends up to or near the soil surface, with a lid on top that can be opened without excavation. Without one, the tank's access point sits several inches to a couple of feet underground, buried under sod or landscaping, and every single service visit starts with someone digging down to find and expose it.

Manhole covers must be large enough for real pumping access, and under Nebraska's state septic code, for example, a septic tank manhole must be at least 12 inches in diameter, according to [University of Nebraska-Lincoln Extension's septic tank design guidance](https://extensionpublications.unl.edu/assets/html/g1473/build/g1473.htm), which also notes that a riser extending to or within six inches of the soil surface is the standard way to make that manhole reachable without excavation each time. Most state codes follow a similar logic even where exact dimensions differ.

## How much does a septic tank riser kit cost?

| Item | Typical cost (2026) |
|---|---|
| DIY riser kit (24-inch, adapter + sections + locking lid) | $150 to $300 |
| Basic 16 to 20-inch riser kit | $100 to $200 |
| Professional installation, parts and labor, per riser | $300 to $600 |
| Digging fee avoided per future pump-out visit | $50 to $200 |

A complete 24-inch kit from a septic supply house, including the tank adapter, riser sections, and a locking lid, commonly runs $150 to $200 for the parts alone, with smaller 16 or 20-inch kits running less. Professional installation, which adds excavation to expose the existing lid, fitting the adapter, and backfilling, typically brings the total to $300 to $600 per riser once labor is included; if you're doing both the inlet and outlet access points at the same visit, expect roughly double the material cost with some labor savings from the shared trip.

Given that a $50 to $200 digging fee applies to every pump-out, inspection, and filter cleaning on a buried tank, a $300 to $600 riser installation typically pays for itself within two to three service visits and keeps saving money on every one after that.

## What types of riser kits are available?

Risers come in a few standard diameters, most commonly 16, 20, and 24 inches, sized to match the tank's existing manhole opening. Plastic risers (usually PVC or high-density polyethylene) are the most common choice for DIY installation: lighter, easier to cut and fit, and less expensive than concrete. Concrete risers cost more and are heavier to install, but offer roughly a 40-year service life and more resistance to cracking under vehicle weight or heavy foot traffic.

Locking lids are worth paying extra for regardless of material, since a secured riser lid at grade is far more likely to be a magnet for curious kids or pets than a buried, hidden access point ever was. Manufacturers like Tuf-Tite and Polylok are common names in the plastic riser category and typically include locking hardware as part of a complete kit rather than sold separately.

## Is installing a riser a DIY job or does it need a professional?

A hand-dug riser installation is a realistic weekend DIY project for a homeowner comfortable with some digging: expect roughly 1 to 2 hours of digging to expose the existing lid, another 30 minutes to stack and glue or seal the riser sections, and about 15 minutes to backfill around the new riser once it's set and sealed.

Where it gets more complicated is depth and existing landscaping. A tank buried deeper than a couple of feet, or one under a deck, patio, or mature landscaping, is a job better handled by a professional with the right equipment, since improper backfilling or a poorly sealed riser-to-tank connection can let groundwater into the tank or let odors and gases escape at the surface.

## What should you check before buying a riser kit?

Measure your tank's existing manhole opening before ordering, since risers are sized to specific diameters and a mismatched kit either won't seal properly or won't fit at all. Confirm the height you need by measuring from the top of the existing lid to the desired grade level; risers are typically sold in stackable sections so you can combine them to reach the right height rather than needing an exact single-piece match.

Check your state or county septic code before installing, since some jurisdictions have specific requirements for riser materials, lid security, or maximum height above grade. A quick call to your local health department, the same one that would review a full drain field replacement, can confirm you're within code before you start digging.

## Does a riser affect anything besides pump-out convenience?

Yes, indirectly. Easier access means service visits happen faster and cost less, which removes one of the small frictions that leads homeowners to put off scheduling maintenance. A riser also makes it realistic to check and clean a septic tank filter between full pump-outs, since reaching the outlet baffle no longer means digging first, and the EPA specifically lists the effluent filter as something that should be checked as part of regular maintenance.

## FAQ

**What is a septic tank riser kit?**
It's a cylindrical extension and lid that raises a septic tank's access opening from below the soil up to grade level, so the tank can be opened for pumping, inspection, or filter cleaning without digging each time.

**How much does a septic tank riser kit cost?**
A DIY kit costs $100 to $300 depending on diameter and material. Professional installation, including labor and excavation to expose the existing lid, typically runs $300 to $600 per riser.

**Does a septic tank riser pay for itself?**
Usually, yes. Buried tank lids typically add $50 to $200 in digging fees to every pumping or inspection visit. A riser installation commonly pays for itself within two to three service visits and saves money on every visit after that.

**Can I install a septic tank riser myself?**
Often, yes, for a tank buried less than a couple of feet deep with clear access. Expect roughly 2 hours of digging and installation time. Deeper tanks or ones under existing landscaping are usually better handled by a professional.

**What size riser kit do I need?**
Riser kits commonly come in 16, 20, and 24-inch diameters, matched to your tank's existing manhole opening. Measure the opening and the depth to grade before ordering, since kits are sold in stackable sections to reach different heights.

## Bottom line

A septic tank riser kit costs $100 to $600 depending on whether you install it yourself or hire it out, and it typically pays for itself within two or three pump-out visits by eliminating the digging fee a buried lid adds every time. For a tank on a normal maintenance schedule, it's one of the few home-service upgrades that reliably saves more than it costs.
