A failing drain field shows up as soggy ground over the trench lines, a sewage smell in the yard, and slow drains in every fixture at once. Repairs like line jetting or soil aeration run $200 to $5,000 and can add years of life if the soil underneath still absorbs water. A full replacement, needed when the soil itself has stopped percolating, runs $5,000 to $20,000 or more.

Which path you're on depends on what actually killed the field, not just how wet the yard looks. A cracked pipe or a root intrusion is a cheap fix. A biomat that has sealed off decades-old trenches, or soil that's saturated end to end, is a replacement conversation. Getting that diagnosis right before paying for a repair is the single biggest cost lever a homeowner has here.

## What are the signs a drain field is failing?

The clearest signs are standing water or spongy ground directly over the trench lines, a sewage odor near the field, and drains backing up or gurgling throughout the house at the same time. A single slow sink points to a plumbing clog; every fixture slowing down together points at the tank or field.

Other tells include grass that's noticeably greener and taller in stripes tracing the trench pattern, since the field is feeding those roots instead of absorbing water. Sewage surfacing on the lawn is a health hazard, not just an eyesore. The [EPA advises keeping people and pets away from any area where wastewater is pooling](https://www.epa.gov/septic/resolving-septic-system-malfunctions) and contacting a professional or your local health department right away.

## What causes a drain field to fail?

Four causes account for most failures we see: biomat buildup, soil compaction, root intrusion, and hydraulic overload from too much water hitting an already-stressed field.

Biomat is the organic layer that naturally forms where effluent meets soil, and a thin layer is normal. Skipped pump-outs let solids escape the tank and thicken that layer until it seals the trench walls shut. According to the EPA, drainfields more than 25 to 30 years old are the ones most likely to have this problem, since the biomat has had decades to thicken and reduce how well the trenches discharge water, which is one reason the agency tells owners of systems that old to start planning for an upgrade before they're in an emergency, according to [EPA guidance on septic system lifespan](https://www.epa.gov/septic/why-maintain-your-septic-system).

Soil compaction happens when something heavy, a parked car, a shed, construction equipment, sits over the field and crushes the air pockets soil needs to absorb water. Roots from water-seeking trees like willows and silver maples crack pipes and choke the gravel bed. Hydraulic overload comes from adding people or water use faster than the field was sized for. A single running toilet can add up to 200 gallons of extra water a day, according to the [EPA's guidance on septic system care](https://www.epa.gov/septic/how-care-your-septic-system), which is enough on its own to drown a marginal field.

## How much does drain field repair cost?

| Repair type | Typical cost (2026) | Best for |
|---|---|---|
| Line jetting | $200 to $500 | Sludge or root clogs in the pipes, soil still absorbing |
| Soil aeration / fracturing | $1,000 to $4,000 | Compacted soil, moderate biomat |
| Distribution box reset/replace | $300 to $1,200 | Uneven flow, one trench flooded and others dry |
| Lateral/pipe replacement | $2,000 to $10,000 | Crushed or root-damaged pipe in one section |
| Full field replacement | $5,000 to $20,000+ | Saturated soil, field-wide failure, end of useful life |

These ranges reflect national averages compiled by Angi and HomeGuide for 2026: Angi puts drain field repair and rejuvenation work at roughly $1,000 to $5,000, with full replacement between $5,000 and $20,000 depending on system type, while HomeGuide's 2026 data shows leach field replacement most commonly landing near $10,000 within a $3,000 to $15,000 range. The EPA's own maintenance economics page frames it simply: repairing or replacing a malfunctioning conventional system typically runs $5,000 to $15,000, against $250 to $500 every three to five years for the maintenance that would have prevented it.

Add-ons that push a replacement toward the top of the range: a perc test to re-evaluate the soil, typically $600 to $2,000, and a county permit, often $450 to $2,000. Both are usually required before a new field can be dug, regardless of what killed the old one.

## How does a contractor decide whether to repair or replace?

A contractor worth hiring starts at the tank, not the field. They'll check sludge and scum depth and inspect the outlet baffle, because a tank that hasn't been pumped in years can cause the exact same symptoms as a failing field, slow drains and backups, without the field being damaged at all.

Next comes the distribution box, the small junction that splits effluent between trench lines. A tilted or clogged D-box sends all the flow to one trench and drowns it while its neighbors stay bone dry, and resetting it for $300 to $1,200 gets misdiagnosed as full field failure more often than it should. Only after the tank and D-box check out does a contractor probe the trenches themselves, checking for standing water and running a camera through accessible lines where possible.

We ask every contractor for their findings in writing before agreeing to anything past a jetting job. A yard that's wet in one visible spot and a $15,000 quote handed over without ever opening the tank is the pattern that costs homeowners the most money.

## When does repair make more sense than replacement?

As a rough rule: a field under 20 years old with one localized problem and soil that still drains elsewhere is usually a repair candidate. A field over 25 to 30 years old, wet across its whole surface, or one that keeps failing after repairs, is usually past the point where spending more on it makes sense.

A septic inspection with a camera and a soil probe, typically $300 to $650 for a real estate-grade visit, is worth the money before committing to either path. Rule out the cheap causes first, too: a missing or clogged effluent filter lets solids pour straight into the field, and a $200 to $300 filter fix at the tank can stop damage that's still in its early stages.

## What does a full drain field replacement involve?

If replacement is the call, expect a county soil evaluation or perc test first, since the soil's percolation rate decides what type of system you're allowed to install and that decision drives price more than anything else. Sandy, well-draining soil with a deep water table typically gets a conventional gravel or chamber field at the lower end of the cost range. Heavy clay, shallow bedrock, or a high water table usually means a mound system or an aerobic treatment unit, which commonly lands between $10,000 and $20,000 or more.

Installation itself typically takes two to four days, and the yard over the new field will look rough for a season afterward. Get at least two bids, and confirm each one includes the same scope: permit responsibility, excavation, pipe and gravel, and final grading. We've seen quotes for the identical job differ by several thousand dollars once one contractor's bid turned out to exclude grading and seeding that the other included.

## How do you protect a repaired or new field going forward?

The habit that matters most is staying on the EPA's recommended schedule: inspect every 1 to 3 years, pump every 3 to 5 years. That schedule exists specifically because solids that build up past what the tank can hold carry over into the field and restart the biomat problem that caused the failure in the first place.

## FAQ

**How much does drain field repair cost?**
Repairs typically run $200 to $5,000: jetting runs $200 to $500, soil aeration or fracturing runs $1,000 to $4,000, and lateral replacement runs $2,000 to $10,000. Full replacement, when the soil itself has failed, runs $5,000 to $20,000 or more.

**Can a failing drain field be fixed without replacing it?**
Often, yes, if the problem is clogged pipes, roots, or compacted soil rather than saturated ground. Jetting or aeration can restore function for years at a fraction of replacement cost. A camera and soil probe inspection is what tells you which situation you're actually in.

**How long does a drain field last?**
A well-maintained conventional field typically lasts 20 to 30 years. The EPA notes that fields over 25 to 30 years old are the ones most likely to be near the end of their useful life as biomat thickens in the trenches, though regular pumping and keeping vehicles off the field can extend that window.

**Is a soggy yard always a sign of septic failure?**
No. Poor grading, a broken irrigation line, or a naturally high water table can all cause wet spots unrelated to the septic system. The septic-specific signs are wetness concentrated over the trench lines, a sewage smell, and drains slowing down inside the house at the same time.

**Does homeowners insurance cover drain field replacement?**
Usually not. Standard policies exclude failures caused by age, wear, or lack of maintenance, which covers most drain field failures. Sudden accidental damage, like a vehicle crushing a pipe, is sometimes covered. Check your policy rather than assuming either way.

## Bottom line

A drain field rarely fails without warning. Catching the signs early, soggy ground, odor, and slow drains together, and getting a real diagnosis before paying for repeat repairs, is what turns a $300 jetting job into the fix instead of the first of several failed attempts. Stick to the EPA's inspection and pumping schedule afterward, because that's the cheapest insurance the field will ever get.
