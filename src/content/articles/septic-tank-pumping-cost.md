Septic tank pumping costs $291 to $565 for a typical 1,000-gallon residential tank, with a national average around $428, according to [Angi's 2026 septic pumping cost data](https://www.angi.com/articles/how-much-does-septic-tank-pumping-cost.htm). The final number on your invoice depends mostly on tank size, how hard the tank is to reach, and where you live; a well-maintained tank with a riser at grade in a low-cost region can run under $300, while a buried tank in a high-cost metro can top $700.

Tank size sets the floor, but access and geography move the price more than most homeowners expect. A crew that has to locate a buried lid, dig it out, and haul the dirt back afterward is doing an extra hour of work before the truck even starts pumping, and that labor shows up on the bill regardless of how big the tank is.

## How much does septic tank pumping cost by tank size?

| Tank size | Typical pumping cost |
|---|---|
| 600 to 750 gallons | $250 to $350 |
| 1,000 gallons (most common residential size) | $300 to $500 |
| 1,250 to 1,500 gallons | $400 to $600 |
| 2,000 gallons and up | $700 and up |

A 1,000-gallon tank is the most common size for a three- to four-bedroom home, and it's the size most quoted prices assume by default. If a quote seems unusually low, confirm the tank size it's based on. A quote for a 750-gallon tank applied to a 1,500-gallon system will be wrong by a wide margin once the truck actually shows up.

## What makes the price go up or down?

Four factors explain most of the spread between a $300 pump-out and a $700 one: access to the lid, tank size, regional labor and disposal costs, and whether the visit is scheduled or same-day.

**Access is the single biggest swing factor.** A tank with a riser extending to grade lets a crew open the lid in minutes. A buried tank with no riser and no accurate as-built record can add $100 to $300 in locating and excavation fees before a single gallon is pumped, since the crew has to probe the yard, dig by hand or machine, and refill the hole afterward.

**Region changes the baseline substantially.** Georgia pump-outs typically run $275 to $450, while the San Francisco Bay Area and Los Angeles suburbs commonly run $500 to $800 for the same size tank, reflecting local labor rates, disposal facility fees, and travel distance between jobs. If you're comparing a quote to a national average, check whether that average reflects your region first.

**Emergency service costs 50 to 100 percent more than a scheduled visit.** A routine $400 pump-out can become a $600 to $800 same-day call once a company reroutes a truck outside its normal schedule. This is the most avoidable line item on the list, since it only exists once maintenance has been pushed past the point of choice.

## What's a real worked example?

A household with a 1,000-gallon tank and a riser already at grade, paying $350 every 4 years, spends about $88 a year on pumping. A neighboring household with a buried 1,500-gallon tank paying $600 every 3 years, once you add locating and excavation fees, spends closer to $200 a year for a similarly sized system. The gap between those two households isn't tank size. It's whether someone installed a riser.

## How does pumping cost compare to letting a system fail?

| Scenario | Typical cost |
|---|---|
| Scheduled pumping, every 3 to 5 years | $300 to $500 per visit |
| Emergency pumping (deferred maintenance) | $500 to $900 per visit |
| Drain field repair after tank neglect | $3,000 to $15,000+ |
| Full septic system replacement | $10,000 to $30,000+ |

[Age, missed pumping, and gradual deterioration together are among the most common causes of premature septic failure](https://content.ces.ncsu.edu/why-do-septic-systems-fail), according to NC State Extension's review of septic system failures. A $400 pump-out every four years is a rounding error next to a drain field replacement, which is the outcome deferred pumping most often leads to.

## How often do you actually need to pump?

The [EPA recommends pumping the average household septic tank every 3 to 5 years](https://www.epa.gov/septic/why-maintain-your-septic-system), with the interval depending on tank size, household size, and how much wastewater the home generates day to day. A smaller household with an oversized tank can often stretch toward the 5-year mark. A large family running a smaller tank, or a home with a garbage disposal adding solids to the tank, may need service closer to every 2 to 3 years.

## Are there ways to lower the cost without cutting corners?

Yes, three things reliably help. Get two or three quotes for anything beyond an easy, scheduled job, since prices for an identical tank size can differ by a couple hundred dollars once you account for a company's base location and current schedule. Install a riser to grade the first time a buried tank gets located, so every future visit skips the excavation fee. And stay on a calendar-based schedule instead of waiting for symptoms, since scheduled pumping is priced lower than emergency service almost everywhere.

## FAQ

**How much does it cost to pump a septic tank?**
Most homeowners pay $300 to $500 for a standard 1,000-gallon tank, with a national average around $428. Larger tanks, buried lids, and high-cost regions can push the price to $700 or more.

**What is the average septic tank pumping cost in the US?**
The national average is approximately $428, with typical bids landing between $291 and $565, based on 2026 Angi cost data. Regional costs vary widely, from around $275 in parts of the South to $800 in high-cost West Coast metros.

**How much does it cost to pump a 1,000-gallon septic tank?**
Expect $300 to $500 for a 1,000-gallon tank with reasonable access. That's the most common residential tank size and the one most published price ranges are built around.

**Does septic tank pumping cost more near me than the national average?**
It can, significantly. Labor rates, disposal facility fees, and travel distance between jobs all vary by region, so a company quoting $600 in a high-cost metro isn't necessarily overcharging compared to a $350 quote in a lower-cost region.

**Is it cheaper to pump my own septic tank?**
DIY pumping isn't realistic for most homeowners: it requires a vacuum truck or trailer, a state or local disposal permit for the septage, and knowledge of how to avoid damaging the tank or baffles. The labor cost you're paying for includes proper disposal at a licensed facility, which most homeowners can't access on their own.

## Bottom line

Budget $300 to $500 for a standard 1,000-gallon tank on a normal schedule, and expect that number to move with tank size, access, and region rather than any one factor alone. The real cost lever isn't finding the cheapest single quote, it's staying on a 3-to-5-year pumping schedule so you never pay the emergency premium or the drain field repair bill that deferred maintenance eventually triggers.
