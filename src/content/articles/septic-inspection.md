A septic inspection is a professional check of your tank, baffles, effluent filter, and drain field to confirm the system is working and catch problems before they become failures. A routine maintenance inspection costs $150 to $300 and takes 30 to 60 minutes. A real estate transaction inspection, which includes pumping the tank and a written report, runs $300 to $650.

Which one you need depends on why you're calling. A homeowner sticking to a maintenance schedule needs the routine version. A buyer, seller, or lender needs the full transaction inspection, because it's the one that produces documentation a bank or attorney will actually accept. Knowing the difference before you call around saves you from paying for more, or less, than the situation requires.

## What does a septic inspector actually check?

A full inspection covers the septic tank, the inlet and outlet baffles, the effluent filter, the distribution box, and the drain field. The inspector locates and opens the tank, measures the depth of the scum and sludge layers, checks that both baffles are intact, and looks at the tank walls and lids for cracks or structural wear.

At the distribution box, they confirm effluent is splitting evenly between trench lines rather than pooling in one and starving the others. In the drain field itself, they probe for standing water and check for surfacing effluent, sunken ground, or unusually lush grass over the trenches, all signs the soil has stopped absorbing water the way it should. On a real estate-grade inspection, the tank is also pumped as part of the visit, since that's the only way to see the tank floor and confirm the baffles are actually in one piece.

## How much does a septic inspection cost?

| Inspection type | Typical cost | What's included |
|---|---|---|
| Routine maintenance check | $150 to $300 | Visual check of tank, baffles, filter; no pump-out |
| Real estate / transfer inspection | $300 to $650 | Pump-out, full component check, flow test, written report |
| Add-on camera inspection | $125 to $500 | Camera run through drain field lines, typically bundled into a real estate inspection |

These figures are consistent across the two largest cost-data sources for the trade: Angi's 2026 report and HomeAdvisor's 2026 cost guide both place routine visits in the $150 to $300 range and real estate-transaction inspections between $300 and $650, with the gap explained mainly by the pump-out and formal report a lender or attorney will require. A camera inspection isn't standard on every visit but is worth requesting on any pre-purchase inspection, since it's the only way to actually see inside the drain field lines rather than infer their condition from the surface.

## How long does a septic inspection take?

A routine visual check runs 30 to 60 minutes once the inspector has located and opened the tank. A full real estate inspection, which adds pumping, a flow test, and a written report, typically takes closer to 2 to 3 hours from arrival to a completed report. Locating the tank if there's no riser or as-built diagram on file adds time to either version, sometimes an hour or more if the lid is buried and unmarked.

## How often do you actually need one?

The EPA recommends inspecting a typical household septic system every 1 to 3 years, with pumping on a separate 3-to-5-year cycle depending on tank size and household water use, according to [EPA guidance on septic system maintenance](https://www.epa.gov/septic/why-maintain-your-septic-system). Alternative or mechanical systems, aerobic treatment units, mound systems, and pressure-distribution setups, need inspection annually, since they have pumps, blowers, and controls that can fail between visits in a way a conventional gravity system doesn't.

Real estate transactions are the other trigger. Most lenders and a growing number of state and county health departments require a septic inspection before closing on a property with an onsite system, separate from and in addition to any routine schedule the current owner has been following.

## What's the difference between a septic inspection and a pumping visit?

A pumping visit empties the tank. An inspection evaluates whether the whole system, tank, baffles, filter, distribution box, and drain field, is functioning correctly. The two overlap because a real estate inspection includes a pump-out as part of getting an accurate look at the tank, but a routine pump-out on its own doesn't produce the same written assessment of the drain field or component condition that a formal inspection does.

If you've had regular pumping but never a full inspection, you have a tank that's been emptied on schedule with no documented picture of whether the drain field underneath it is still healthy. Those are different questions, and only one of them tells you if a failure is coming.

## What should you ask before hiring an inspector?

Confirm the inspector is licensed or certified to perform septic inspections in your state, since requirements and titles (registered sanitarian, licensed evaluator, certified inspector) vary by state health department. Ask whether the quoted price includes pumping, a written report, and a drain field probe, or whether any of those are billed separately, since "inspection" gets used loosely across the industry the same way "pumping" and "cleaning" do.

Ask for the report format in advance if you're buying or selling a home. A lender-acceptable report needs to document tank condition, baffle condition, sludge and scum measurements, and drain field observations in writing, not just a verbal pass or fail.

## FAQ

**How much does a septic inspection cost?**
A routine maintenance inspection costs $150 to $300. A real estate transaction inspection, which includes pumping and a written report, runs $300 to $650. Adding a camera run through the drain field lines typically adds $125 to $500.

**What do septic inspectors look for?**
Inspectors check the tank's structural condition, scum and sludge depth, inlet and outlet baffle condition, the effluent filter, the distribution box, and the drain field for signs of surfacing effluent or saturated soil. A real estate inspection also includes pumping the tank as part of getting an accurate reading.

**How often should I get a septic inspection?**
The EPA recommends every 1 to 3 years for a conventional system, and annually for alternative or mechanical systems like aerobic treatment units or mound systems, which have more components that can fail between visits.

**Do I need a septic inspection to sell my house?**
In most cases, yes. Most lenders and many state or county health departments require a septic inspection report before closing on a property with an onsite system, and it's separate from whatever routine maintenance schedule the seller has been following.

**Is a septic inspection the same as pumping?**
No. Pumping empties the tank. An inspection evaluates the whole system's condition, including the drain field, and produces a written assessment. A real estate-grade inspection includes a pump-out, but a routine pump-out alone doesn't include a full inspection report.

## Bottom line

A septic inspection costs $150 for a routine check and up to $650 for a full real estate-transaction visit, and the two aren't interchangeable with a pumping appointment. Stick to the EPA's 1-to-3-year inspection cycle for a conventional system, go annual if you have an alternative system with mechanical parts, and always budget for a full inspection with a written report before buying or selling a home with a septic system.
